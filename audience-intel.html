How to read this

This diagnostic covers the LinkedIn ad account as it ran January 1 to June 22, 2026 — the period before Impactable's engagement went live (MSA effective June 3, onboarding June 22). Every number here reflects UnderDefense's own prior targeting decisions: the activity that, in the client's words, "did not provide outcomes."

We are presenting it deliberately. The data shows the channel was not the problem. The targeting was. The right buyers engaged at two to three times the account average when they were reached; they simply were not the audience that received the budget. That is a fixable problem, and fixing it is exactly what the rebuilt net-new-led, ICP-disciplined structure is designed to do.

Tracking status — read before efficiency claims

On-site conversion tracking is unreliable in this baseline data. Conversions are platform-reported and last-touch, and privacy-conscious security buyers evade standard pixels (the reason server-side tracking is a Day-1 workstream). Across the period, recorded conversions are too sparse and inconsistent to anchor a cost-per-lead verdict. The primary efficiency signal used throughout this report is engagement quality (CTR) cross-referenced against ICP fit, not cost-per-conversion. Closed-loop lead quality becomes measurable once HubSpot and server-side tracking are connected.

What this means for our strategy

The segment split is the proof behind warm-first, ICP-disciplined targeting

Our refined direction leads with net-new pipeline (warm retargeting as the efficiency layer) and a tightly defined ICP: security leaders and Security Managers / SecOps Directors at 500–3,000-employee US firms — Fintech first, then Tech/SaaS and Healthcare. This baseline shows why that direction is right, not arbitrary. The prior account inverted it: budget concentrated on generic engineering, broad seniorities, and sub-500 firms, while the actual ICP engaged best on scraps.

Direction holds, sharpened: we keep the ICP definition exactly as written and add campaign-level enforcement — seniority and title gating toward security roles, hard sub-500 and military exclusions from Day 1. No change to the thesis; a concrete fix to execution the prior setup did not apply.

Key findings

What the segments are telling us

Critical — targeting leak

Four of five dollars missed the security buyer

Primary-ICP security executives (CISO, VP Sec, Director of Security) took only ~5% of title-level spend; security practitioners another ~14%. The single largest title bucket was generic engineering / IT at ~21%, and off-ICP roles (sales, marketing, recruiting) absorbed ~16%. The audience that engaged best was not the audience that got funded.

Critical — firmographic + exclusion leak

30% of spend hit companies they told us to exclude

The brief is explicit: no-go under 500 employees. Yet 30% of size-attributed spend landed at sub-500 firms, roughly half of that at companies of 50 or fewer. Separately, $1,367 reached Military & Protective Services, another stated exclusion. Exclusions were not enforced at the campaign level.

Opportunity — underfunded winners

The right titles posted the best CTRs on scraps

CISO (0.99%), Director of Cyber Security (1.11%), and the Cyber Security Engineer / Analyst / Specialist cluster (0.62–0.77%) all beat the 0.38% account average by 1.6–3x. These are precisely the bottom-up entry roles the strategy targets. Fund them.

Watch — vertical signal buried

Named verticals barely register; targeting was broad

A large untargeted / "unknown industry" block absorbed ~$16K-equivalent of impressions, while named priority verticals sat at 1–2% each (Financial Services, Healthcare, Insurance). The account was not segmented by vertical, so vertical-level lead quality was not readable. Our build splits these out.

What this means for our strategy

Two findings become two of our fastest, most defensible early wins

The targeting leak and the firmographic/exclusion leak are not just diagnoses, they are the efficiency recovery we lead the relationship with. Reallocating the ~four-in-five dollars that missed the security buyer, and shutting the ~30% that hit excluded company sizes, frees budget without asking for a dollar more. That is exactly the kind of "spot and minimize waste" move our operating model promises, and it is visible in Month 1.

Direction holds, sharpened: the underfunded-winners finding tells us where the freed budget goes — concentrate on the security titles already proven to engage. The buried-vertical finding validates our plan to split campaigns by named vertical (tech / financial services / healthcare) so lead quality finally becomes readable per segment. Both map straight onto the strategy already written.

What this means for our strategy

These four personas are the spine of the campaign architecture

Our strategy calls for a full-funnel build pairing founder thought-leadership at the top with case-study and proof retargeting below. These personas tell us who sits in each layer: the Security Chief is the funded center of gravity (founder-led + named proof), the Practitioner is the volume layer that carries the idea upward (practical, checklist content), the Sponsor gets committee coverage but a capped budget, and the Misfire defines the exclusion list.

Direction holds, sharpened: no change to the funnel model. The diagnostic simply fills it with evidence-backed audiences instead of assumptions, and reweights spend away from the Sponsor (CTO was the single largest title spend) toward the Chief and Practitioner who actually engaged.

Saturation analysis

Frequency & penetration on the narrow lists Last 92 days. Healthy LinkedIn prospecting frequency is ~2–5x over a flight. The competitor-ABM and retargeting pools were too small to absorb spend without fatiguing the exact buyers they targeted.

Campaign (narrow-targeted)ReachedFrequencyPenetrationSpendRead

So what

Four campaigns ran at 8–18x frequency against pools under 1,000 people. The MDR LinkedIn-page retargeting campaign hit 17.8x frequency on 304 people with 56% penetration — more than half that audience saw the same ad roughly eighteen times. In a privacy-sensitive security audience, that is not retargeting, it is fatigue: rising costs and annoyed buyers.

Direction: consolidate the competitor slivers into broader buyer-group campaigns with hard frequency caps, and lead with the far larger warm pools (~11–12K parked leads, open-opportunity committees) that can actually absorb spend. A weekly penetration / frequency check becomes a standing optimization on this account, because the prior instinct was to over-narrow.

How this influences our stated strategy

The data validates the direction. It does not change it. Read against the strategy already set, this baseline is confirmation, not course-correction. Below: what holds, what sharpens, and the one thing still unmeasured.

Confirmed — warm-first spine

An 80/20 warm-to-cold split is the right call

The prior account ran cold and scattered and fatigued its tiny lists at up to 17.8x frequency. The warm pools (~11–12K parked leads and open-opportunity committees) are an order of magnitude larger and can absorb spend without saturation. Leading warm is both lower-risk and faster to a visible win. No change.

Confirmed — ICP definition

Security leaders + SecOps at 500–10K firms is correct

Every dimension points the same way: security titles out-engaged the account average by 1.6–3x, while sub-500 firms and off-ICP functions drained budget. The ICP we wrote is exactly where the signal is. No change — now enforced at the campaign level.

Sharpened — execution detail

Three concrete additions to the build

(1) Hard exclusions from Day 1 (sub-500, military, non-profit). (2) Frequency caps plus a weekly penetration check as a standing optimization, because the prior instinct was to over-narrow. (3) Campaigns split by named vertical so lead quality is readable. None of these alter the thesis; they tighten how we deliver it.

Still unmeasured — state plainly

Two gaps cap what we can claim today

Google is not connected through Windsor, so the channel the client said showed life is unmeasured — half the mandate. And closed-loop lead quality (HubSpot + server-side) is not yet live, so every verdict here is engagement-based, not conversion-based. Both are pre-launch items, not strategy changes, but they bound today's confidence and should be named in the room.

Bottom line for the client conversation

We are not changing the plan. We are showing the receipts behind it.

The strategy UnderDefense signed off on was built on judgment. This diagnostic converts that judgment into evidence: the channel was not the problem, the targeting was, and the fix is the net-new-led, ICP-disciplined, exclusion-enforced build already designed. That is the confident, defensible story to lead with — it reframes Impactable from "another agency promising leads" to "the team that already found the leak before spending a dollar."

Next to unlock the full picture: connect Google through Windsor and stand up closed-loop tracking, so the next iteration measures both channels on real lead quality rather than engagement proxies.
